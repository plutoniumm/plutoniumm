Base Conversion

To convert from base 10 to base 2: divide by 2 repeatedly and record remainders. 420÷2=210 r 0210÷2=105 r 0105÷2=52 r 152÷2=26 r 026÷2=13 r 013÷2=6 r 16÷2=3 r 03÷2=1 r 1 \begin{align*} 420 \div 2 &= 210\ &r\ 0\\ 210 \div 2 &= 105\ &r\ 0\\ 105 \div 2 &= 52\ &r\ 1\\ 52 \div 2 &= 26\ &r\ 0\\ 26 \div 2 &= 13\ &r\ 0\\ 13 \div 2 &= 6\ &r\ 1\\ 6 \div 2 &= 3\ &r\ 0\\ 3 \div 2 &= 1\ &r\ 1 \end{align*} 420÷2210÷2105÷252÷226÷213÷26÷23÷2​=210 =105 =52 =26 =13 =6 =3 =1 ​r 0r 0r 1r 0r 0r 1r 0r 1​ Then we read the remainders from bottom to top (incl. the last quotient), which gives us the binary number 110100100. as we count the number of times we divided by 2 from the bottom up. We can also go in the other direction, from base 2 to base 7. The easiest way to do this is to go to base 10 first, then to base 7. 420÷7=60 r 060÷7=8 r 48÷7=1 r 1 \begin{align*} 420 \div 7 &= 60\ &r\ 0\\ 60 \div 7 &= 8\ &r\ 4\\ 8 \div 7 &= 1\ &r\ 1\\ \end{align*} 420÷760÷78÷7​=60 =8 =1 ​r 0r 4r 1​ which again, including the last quotient, gives us the base 7 number 1140.

With that out of the way, we can now look at some more interesting bases. In general as long as we have some base bbb, we use the digits 0 to b−1b-1b−1 to represent the number, with the obvious exception of 0.

Removing Constraints

So far, above we set some very arbitrary rules on what digits we can use for what base. In reality however those are the standard preferences for the given bases, there don't actually exist any constraints on what base we can use. Consider using the symbols of base 16, i.e, [0→9,A→F][0 \rightarrow 9, A \rightarrow F][0→9,A→F], but in base 7. Then the number F1 would be 15⋅(7)1+1⋅(7)0=15⋅(7.00)+1⋅(1.00)=1061015 \cdot (7)^1 + 1 \cdot (7)^0 = 15 \cdot (7.00) + 1 \cdot (1.00) = 106_{10}15⋅(7)1+1⋅(7)0=15⋅(7.00)+1⋅(1.00)=10610​. There are actually no limits to what digit we choose every step.
