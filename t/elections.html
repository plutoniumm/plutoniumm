Hamilton’s Method

The way this was proposed to be solved, it keeps rounding up from the number closest to the next integer until whoever else is left gets rounded down, even if they’re closer to the next integer. This is called the Hamilton’s Method. So, in this case, we would round up Reasongrad and Analyticaland and round down Logicia. So we get two seats for Reasongrad and Analyticaland and one for Logicia. This method is precisely what was also first proposed for Alabama in 1792.

So if some state needs 0.3 more seats to round up to 3 (from 2.7), they will be rounded up to 3 even if there exists a state with 26.6 seats that need 0.4 more seats to round up to 27. This allows for what would be ‘fair’ rounding since a given state is closest to the next integer. The problem arises when we add just one more seat. Then which number is closest to the next integer jumps around completely randomly. Consider the below actual example from Alabama (sorted by descending remaining fraction)

This was actually the method they went with from 1792 to 1840. The one massive problem with this issue is as we decrease the denominator since it is a fraction, larger numbers increase faster. So say 1000/50 = 20 and 1000/40 = 25, whereas 100/50 = 2 and 100/40 = 2.5; notice how a state with a 1000 population gained 5 seats, whereas the 100 population state gained only 0.5 seats, which may or may not even get. So the larger states get more seats faster than the smaller states. This is called the Jefferson’s Method.

Adam & Webster Methods

Adam’s method is exactly the same as Jefferson’s method, except rather than force round down and then slowly decrease the denominator, we force round UP and slowly increase the denominator. This is called the Adam’s Method. The Jefferson method favours the larger states, and the Adam’s method favours the smaller states (for the same reason, since large numbers move faster, the larger states also lose seats faster).

Webster’s method is again the same as Adam’s & Jefferson’s, except rather than force round up or down, we round to the nearest integer like a normal human. This is called the Webster’s Method.

We can clearly see here how Reasongrad, with the most votes, won this election, but they made a leader which, in fact, 78% of people did not want. So this is technically a minority rule system since all states are minorities. So we have effectively been unfair to 78% of the population. We can see how this system gets worse when there are more and more cities. If there were 20 candidates, then someone with only 6% of the votes could win simply because of the most votes despite being only favoured by 6% of the population.

This method, unfortunately, also forces a two-party system. In every election, they see what happens, at the fact that they have abysmal results and eventually, the voters decide to go with the candidates they LEAST DISAGREE within an endless cycle till there are only two candidates. Given enough time, all First Past the Post systems will eventually become two-party systems. This is often called the Duverger’s Law. This has been clearly seen in several countries like the US, UK, India, Canada, Australia, etc. There do, however, exist some rare exceptions like the Philippines, where since 1987, no party has been able to control the House of Representatives

This eventually has another problem where the majority of the voter base who did not originally want the now 2 majority parties eventually become disinterested in the political process and stop voting in what is often termed Voter Apathy where the voters stop wanting to vote because they feel their vote does not matter or does not meaningfully express who they want as a candidate.

Gerrymandering

Gerrymandering is the process of redrawing the borders of a state to favour a particular party. This is often done by the party in power to ensure they stay in power. This is often done by splitting the opposition party’s voter base into multiple districts so that they have a majority in all districts but not a majority in any one district. This is often called Cracking & Packing.

Consider the following example. let us say, Newton, Einstein, and Hawking are 3 candidates, and there are 3 voters who want Newton, 3 Who want Einstein and 4 who want Hawking. Now if we split the district voters into the following 2 ways

  • Constituency 1: Newton, Newton, Newton, Hawking, Hawking
  • Constituency 2: Einstein, Einstein, Einstein, Hawking, Hawking

We can now see how Newton and Einstein have a majority in both constituencies despite each being less than Hawking, and in the end, Hawking voters would have been eliminated completely. Gerrymandering gives whoever draws the lines an unfair advantage on the system.

The Spoiler Effect

The Spoiler Effect is when a third-party candidate takes votes away from a major party candidate, causing a loss. This is often used as an argument against third parties. So rather than actually helping us choose more candidates, it just becomes a way to play more politics. So if Einstein gives Hawking money to stand in an election and then says it proposes SIMILAR but not the same ideologies as Newton, some of Newton’s voters will now vote for Hawking, thus taking away some chance of Newton winning. This is often called the Spoiler Effect.

So far now, we need 3 things to make a better system

  • We should be able to vote for who we want
  • No Gerrymandering
  • More parties are better
  • And it should be open to newcomers (IMPORTANT)

Multiple Runoff

This system is similar to the Instant Runoff Voting system, but rather than eliminating the last candidate immediately and just recalculating the votes; we have multiple rounds of voting. So we have the first round, we vote again with 3 candidates, then we vote again with 2 candidates and so on. Or we can have a system similar to Brazil where we have 2 rounds of voting (assuming there is no clear >50% in one shot), the first round is similar to the Instant Runoff Voting system, and the second round is a Winner Takes All system. This is called the Multiple Runoff system.

Conclusions & Caveats

Possibilities

It is, in theory, mathematically possible to show that for any system, there are some criteria that a reasonable human would include in the idea of ‘fairness’ that gets left out (Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem).

  • Mutual Majority
  • Non Dictatorship
  • Condorcet Criterion
  • Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives
  • Monotonicity
  • Etc, head over to the Technical Definition of Arrow’s Impossibility Theorem for more details.

A simpler way to say this would be with the results of Gibbard’s Theorem (which is a consequence of Arrow’s Theorem) which states that for any process of collective decision, one of the following MUST, hold

  • The process is dictatorial
  • The process is susceptible to tactical voting
  • The process limits the possible outcomes to TWO options only

Comparisons

Of all the systems we have seen, we can clearly see how each of them have their own advantages and disadvantages. So usually, when selecting a political system, it is not a matter of what’s best but what is least bad. So say in a large democracy like India, it may not even be feasible to do Multiple Runoff in the first place, or it may not be fair to do something else somewhere else. So it is not a matter of what’s best but what’s least bad since we know there are inevitably going to be SOME problems

Caveats

There is SOME influence on how each system itself is implemented, say,

Whether or not voting is compulsory in a system or not, so there are 4 types of systems

  • Enforced Compulsory Voting: Ex. Australia, Brazil etc
  • Non Enforced Compulsory Voting: Ex. Turkey, Belgium etc
  • Partially Compulsory Voting (men only): Ex. Egypt, Lybia etc
  • Voluntary Voting: Ex. India, USA etc

Or whether or not reservations exist, the most significant example by far would be India, where minorities and reserved castes have a significant influence on the outcome of the election. This is often such a deal breaker that parties tend to get commonly accused of ‘Vote Bank Politics’ or ‘Minority Appeasement’ where they try to appease a certain group of people to get their votes. There are also other countries like Morocco where the reservations are not only by caste or gender but also by age, they have 30 reserved seats for people under 40, and it is well known that generally younger people lean left-er than older people and they gradually become more conservative as they age.

So a few things done occasionally in some systems are Gerrymandering around the likelihood of voting of a region or demographics, say if it is compulsory to vote for men and a region favours you, then skew for that, or if women dislike your party, then split apart regions where women tend to vote high. While these are generally things that do make a difference, I decided not to go into too much detail about them since they can be deduced as a special case of the systems we have already seen.

There has also been no mention of strategies political parties use to target certain demographics; the fact that that is done is implicit in ways to game any system.
